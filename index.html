Section 01 - The artists

Australia's biggest names
on Spotify

From western Sydney housing estates to European festival stages, Australian artists have quietly been conquering global streaming. Between 2017 and 2021, 49 Australian artists accumulated over 7 billion streams outside of Australia — a number that would have seemed impossible a decade ago. This is the story of how they did it, who led the charge, and what the data reveals about the difference between local fame and global impact.

The Kid LAROI — raised in Waterloo, Sydney — accumulated over 3.9 billion global streams, charting in 68 countries. His rise from 2020 to 2021 is the fastest breakout in this entire dataset. Born Charlton Howard, he was mentored by Juice WRLD and signed internationally at just 16. By 2021, he had more global streams than the next three Australian artists combined.

Section 02 - Home base

How they perform
back in Australia

Global success rarely happens overnight. Most artists build a home fanbase first — touring locally, getting played on triple j, climbing the Australian charts before the world takes notice. But the data reveals something surprising: some of Australia's biggest global stars barely charted at home. The question is — does Australian success predict global success? The answer, as you'll see, is complicated.

The chart below shows every Australian artist's peak chart rank on the Australian Spotify Top 200. The further left the dot, the higher they peaked — rank 1 means they hit number one. The size of each dot represents how many days they spent on the Australian charts across the full five years. A big dot far left means consistently dominant at home. A small dot far right means they barely registered with Australian listeners at all.

5 Seconds of Summer and The Kid LAROI both hit #1 in Australia — but notice the dot sizes. 5SOS spent over 1,300 days on the Australian charts across five years. LAROI's dot is much smaller — he peaked at #1 but didn't sustain a long home chart presence. Meanwhile, artists like Vance Joy and Ocean Alley spent years consistently charting in Australia, quietly building loyal local fanbases without ever breaking the global top tier.

PNAU's story is fascinating — they peaked heavily in 2017–2018 with Chameleon and then essentially disappeared from the charts. Hooligan Hefs emerged from nowhere in 2019 and built steady momentum through to 2021 — a slow burn that never exploded but never faded either. Ruel peaked sharply in 2019 at just 17 years old before levelling off — the hallmark of early viral success that couldn't be sustained.

Kylie Minogue, Sia and The Kid LAROI have less than 5% of their total streams from Australia — they are effectively international artists who happen to be Australian. On the other end, Hilltop Hoods, Spacey Jane, Chillinit and Gang of Youths have less than 2% of streams from outside Australia. These are distinctly Australian artists whose music resonates deeply at home but hasn't crossed cultural borders — yet. Neither path is better. They're just completely different careers.

went global

The Spotify Top 200 is published daily for over 70 countries. When an Australian artist appears on any of those charts, it leaves a data trail. The map below aggregates every stream from every Australian artist across every country in the dataset — giving us a global heat signature of Australian music's reach between 2017 and 2021. The darker the green, the more Australian music was consumed there.

Where Aussie artists charted around the world

Total streams from Australian artists by country · Spotify Top 200 · 2017-2021 · Hover for details · Log colour scale

The English-speaking world dominates. The United States leads with over 1 billion streams, followed by the United Kingdom, Canada, and Ireland. This reflects both language accessibility and the cultural pipeline between Australia and the Anglosphere — Australian artists tend to break into British and American markets before anywhere else. Europe shows strong numbers in the Netherlands, Germany and the Nordics — countries with historically high Spotify penetration and appetite for English-language pop. Most of Asia, Africa and South America remain dark — Australian music has not yet meaningfully penetrated these markets.

It's worth noting what this map doesn't show. A country with few streams isn't necessarily uninterested — Spotify itself had limited availability in many Asian and African markets during 2017–2021. The blank regions may reflect platform availability as much as musical preference.

Section 04 - The breakout

Home heroes vs
world conquerors

We've seen who charted at home and who charted globally. Now the question is: are these the same artists? The answer reveals a fundamental split in Australian music — a divide between artists who built careers within Australia and those who used Australia as a launchpad to somewhere much bigger. The charts in this section map that divide in three different ways.

Each dot in the scatter plot below represents one Australian artist. Their position on the x-axis shows their total Australian home streams — how big they are locally. Their position on the y-axis shows their total global streams — how far they travelled. Both axes use a log scale because the differences between artists are so extreme that a linear scale would compress most artists into an unreadable cluster. The size of each dot shows how many countries they charted in — the bigger the dot, the more widely they spread.

Look at the cluster of artists along the bottom-right — high home streams, almost no global streams. These are Australia's most beloved local artists: Hilltop Hoods, Ocean Alley, Spacey Jane. The top-right corner — high in both dimensions — belongs almost entirely to The Kid LAROI and 5 Seconds of Summer. True crossover success at that scale is extraordinarily rare.

That spike in mid-2021 belongs entirely to The Kid LAROI. The song was STAY, featuring Justin Bieber — released July 9, 2021. It debuted at #1 in Australia, the US, the UK and 15 other countries simultaneously. In a single month, LAROI generated more streams than Sia had accumulated across the entire previous year. Every other artist's line barely registers on the same scale. This is what a global moment looks like in streaming data.

Sia's consistency is unmatched — she charted in 60+ countries every single year from 2017 to 2021, the only Australian artist to achieve that. Kylie Minogue shows similar consistency. The Kid LAROI appears only from 2020 — but immediately charts in more countries than most artists ever reach. Some careers are marathons. Some are meteors. The data doesn't judge — it just shows the shape of the journey.

Section 05 — The Takeaway

What the data
tells us about
Australian music

Five years of Spotify data reveals a clear divide in Australian music. A tiny handful of artists crossed over to global audiences — and when they did, they did it in a big way. The majority built devoted local fanbases that never translated internationally. Neither path is a failure. They are simply two completely different definitions of success.

The numbers behind the story: 49 Australian artists. 7 billion global streams. 69 countries reached. But just 6 artists account for over 90% of all global streams. Australian music's global footprint is vast — but it rests on very few shoulders.

The story in six numbers

Out of 49 Australian artists - how many reached each milestone? Each disc fills proportionally to show the share of artists.

The Kid LAROI went from Waterloo, Sydney to 68 countries in two years. Hilltop Hoods spent five years as Australia's most beloved hip-hop act without a single meaningful stream outside the country. Sia charted in 60+ countries every year for five years through sheer consistency. Every artist in this dataset found their own version of success — the data just shows us the shape of it.
